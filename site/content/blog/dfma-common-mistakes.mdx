Design for Manufacturing and Assembly is often treated as a one-time checklist exercise, run just before a design freeze. In practice, the biggest cost drivers are introduced much earlier, when a designer is still exploring geometry and hasn't yet thought about how the part will actually be made.

## 1. Treating fasteners as free

Every additional fastener adds a hole, a torque spec, a possible cross-thread failure, and an assembly step that a technician has to repeat thousands of times. Reviewing fastener count against function — not habit — is one of the highest-leverage DFMA activities available.

## 2. Ignoring tolerance stack-up until final inspection

Tight tolerances specified in isolation, without checking how they stack across an assembly, are one of the most common causes of unplanned rework on a production line. A tolerance stack-up study during the design phase catches this before tooling is cut.

## 3. Designing for the CAD model, not the operator

A part that looks clean in CAD can be nearly impossible to install with a torque wrench in a confined enclosure. Physical mockups or simple 3D-printed fit checks catch this early.

## 4. Overlooking symmetry opportunities

Parts that could be made symmetric — but aren't — introduce the risk of incorrect orientation during assembly, and require the operator to check every time. Small geometry changes can eliminate this entirely.

## 5. Skipping the supplier conversation

Manufacturing engineers who never talk to the people running the CNC machine or the mould press are working from incomplete information. A short review with the manufacturing partner before design freeze regularly surfaces cost reductions that no internal checklist would catch.
