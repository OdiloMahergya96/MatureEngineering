When original drawings for a part are lost — often because the machine builder no longer exists, or the part predates any digital record — reverse engineering is usually proposed as a quick fix: scan the part, extract a model, done. In practice, that is only the first of several steps.

## Scanning captures geometry, not intent

A 3D scan captures the part exactly as it exists today, including wear, prior repairs, and manufacturing variation. It does not tell you which surfaces were functionally critical and which were incidental. An engineer has to interpret the scan against the part's function to reconstruct the *intended* geometry, not just the *as-measured* geometry.

## Tolerances have to be inferred, not measured

You cannot measure a design tolerance directly from a used part — you can only measure its current dimension. Inferring the original tolerance requires understanding the mating parts, the assembly process, and the failure consequences if the tolerance were tighter or looser than assumed.

## Material identification closes the loop

Without a specification, replacing a part with the wrong grade of steel or the wrong polymer can silently reduce fatigue life or chemical resistance. Spectrometric or hardness testing is often necessary to confirm material properties before finalizing a reconstruction.

## The output should be a usable drawing package, not just a CAD file

A reverse-engineered part is only useful if a machine shop or mould maker can quote and build it with confidence. That means GD&T, surface finish callouts, and material specification — the same rigor as an original design, working backward instead of forward.

Done properly, reverse engineering removes a single-source risk permanently, rather than producing a fragile one-off replacement.
