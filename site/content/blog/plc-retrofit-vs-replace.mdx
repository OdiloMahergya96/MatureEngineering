When a piece of automation equipment starts causing reliability issues, the instinct is often to quote a full replacement. That is sometimes the right call — but not as often as it seems.

## Separate the mechanical structure from the control system

A machine's mechanical frame, bearings, and drive components typically have a very different service life than its control system. A structure that is still within its fatigue life but running on an obsolete, unsupported PLC platform is a strong retrofit candidate — replacing controls alone can restore reliability at a fraction of the cost of a new machine.

## Ask what data the plant is actually missing

Many retrofit decisions are driven by more than reliability — plant managers increasingly want OEE data, downtime causes, and maintenance alerts that legacy equipment simply can't produce. A retrofit is often the fastest way to bring an older asset into a modern SCADA environment.

## Quantify the downtime cost of doing nothing

Unplanned downtime on aging equipment is rarely tracked accurately. Before comparing retrofit against replacement cost, it's worth quantifying the actual cost of the current failure rate — this number often changes the decision entirely.

## When replacement is the right call

If the mechanical structure has reached the end of its safe fatigue life, if spare parts for structural components are no longer available at any price, or if production volume has outgrown the machine's physical capacity, replacement is usually the more defensible long-term decision.

A short condition assessment — typically one to two weeks — is usually enough to make this decision with confidence rather than instinct.
